# Firecraft

## 1. Why It Matters

Fire provides heat, light, water purification, signaling capability, and psychological stability. In cold or wet conditions, it is a critical survival multiplier.

Failure to establish fire when needed significantly increases risk of hypothermia, dehydration (via inability to purify water), and reduced morale.


## 2. Principles

### Fire Triangle (Operational)

Fire requires:

- Heat (ignition source)
- Fuel (tinder → kindling → fuel)
- Oxygen (airflow)

Failure in any one component results in failure to ignite or sustain fire.


### Preparation Before Ignition

Most fire failures occur due to poor preparation.

- Tinder must be ready before ignition
- Kindling must be staged
- Fuel must be accessible

Do not attempt ignition until all materials are prepared.


### Progression of Materials

Fire must scale in stages:

1. Tinder (ignites easily)
2. Kindling (sustains initial flame)
3. Fuel (maintains long-term burn)

Skipping stages leads to failure.


### Dry Material Selection

Dryness is more important than size.

- Use interior wood when exterior is wet
- Seek dead standing wood rather than ground material


### Efficiency Over Size

Large fires waste fuel and energy.

- Build fire to meet need (heat, water, signal)
- Maintain control


## 3. Techniques

### Tinder Preparation

Examples:

- Dry grass, bark, or plant fibers
- Feather sticks (processed wood curls)

Tinder must ignite quickly and sustain flame long enough to transition to kindling.


### Fire Lay Types

#### Teepee
- Easy ignition
- Good for quick heat

#### Log Cabin
- Stable structure
- Sustains longer burn

#### Lean-To
- Useful in wind or rain
- Protects flame during ignition


### Ignition Methods

#### Spark-Based
- Ferro rod or flint and steel
- Direct sparks into tinder

#### Flame-Based
- Lighter or matches
- Shield from wind during ignition

#### Friction-Based (Advanced)
- Bow drill or hand drill
- High energy cost and low reliability under stress


### Fire Maintenance

- Add fuel gradually
- Maintain airflow
- Avoid smothering flame


## 4. Field Drills

### Drill: One-Match Fire

- Build a fire using a single ignition attempt

Goal: improve preparation discipline


### Drill: Wet Condition Fire

- Build fire using damp materials

Goal: develop material selection and processing skill


### Drill: Time-Constrained Fire

- Establish fire within a fixed time limit (e.g., 10 minutes)


## 5. Common Mistakes

- Attempting ignition before preparation is complete
- Using damp or insufficient tinder
- Skipping material progression stages
- Building fire too large too quickly
- Ignoring airflow


## 6. Decision Triggers

- Cold or wet conditions → prioritize fire early
- Loss of daylight → establish fire before dark
- Need to purify water → establish sustained fire


## 7. Equipment Notes

- Ferro rod provides reliable ignition in most conditions
- Lighter offers fastest ignition but limited lifespan
- Knife enables creation of feather sticks and dry material access
