# Sheltercraft

## 1. Why It Matters

Shelter is the highest priority in most survival scenarios. Exposure to cold, wind, and moisture can degrade physical and cognitive function rapidly and lead to hypothermia or heat injury.

A functional shelter does not need to be complex. It must reduce exposure, conserve heat, and provide a stable environment for recovery and planning.


## 2. Principles

### Control Heat Loss

Heat is lost through:

- Conduction (ground contact)
- Convection (wind)
- Radiation (body heat loss)
- Evaporation (moisture)

Effective shelter addresses all four.


### Site Selection Matters More Than Construction

A poor location cannot be fixed with better construction.

- Avoid low ground (water collection)
- Avoid exposed ridgelines (wind)
- Use natural windbreaks
- Stay near resources when possible (wood, water)


### Insulation Is Critical

Ground insulation is often more important than overhead cover.

- Use debris, vegetation, or gear
- Maintain separation from cold or wet ground


### Size Matters

Shelters should be small enough to retain heat.

- Larger shelters require more energy to warm
- Build to fit, not for comfort


### Build for Conditions, Not Preference

- Cold/wet: prioritize insulation and wind protection
- Hot/dry: prioritize shade and airflow


## 3. Techniques

### Debris Shelter

- Select dry, elevated location
- Construct ridge pole between supports
- Add rib structure
- Cover with dense debris (minimum 12–18 inches thick)
- Seal gaps to reduce airflow


### Lean-To Shelter

- Position back toward prevailing wind
- Angle roof to shed water
- Use reflector wall if fire is used


### Tarp Shelter (If Available)

- Use simple configurations (lean-to, A-frame)
- Anchor securely to prevent collapse
- Maintain drainage path away from sleeping area


### Snow Shelter

- Dig below surface level to reduce wind exposure
- Create ventilation opening
- Avoid overexertion during construction


## 4. Field Drills

### Drill: Rapid Shelter Build

- Build a functional shelter in under 60 minutes
- Focus on insulation and wind protection


### Drill: Site Selection Walk

- Evaluate multiple potential sites
- Select best based on exposure, drainage, and resources


### Drill: Cold Ground Test

- Sit or lie on ground without insulation
- Add insulation and compare heat retention


## 5. Common Mistakes

- Building too large
- Ignoring ground insulation
- Poor site selection
- Leaving gaps that allow wind penetration
- Overinvesting time in structure vs insulation


## 6. Decision Triggers

- Temperature drop or wind increase → build shelter immediately
- Wet conditions → prioritize insulation over structure complexity
- Less than 2 hours of daylight → begin shelter construction


## 7. Equipment Notes

- Tarp significantly reduces build time
- Paracord improves structural stability
- Knife enables efficient material processing
