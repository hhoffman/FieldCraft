# Priorities in the Field

## 1. Why It Matters

Most survival failures occur in the first hour after a problem is recognized. Individuals either act too quickly without direction or delay action while conditions degrade. Prioritization determines whether time and energy are used effectively or wasted.

Correct priorities are not fixed—they shift based on environment, condition, and available resources. The objective is to address the most immediate threats first while preserving the ability to recover.


## 2. Principles

### The Rule of 3 (Operational Interpretation)

The commonly cited “Rule of 3” is a guideline, not a literal timer:

- 3 minutes without air
- 3 hours without shelter (in extreme exposure)
- 3 days without water
- 3 weeks without food

In practice, this translates to:

- Immediate threats to life take priority
- Exposure is often the fastest killer in the field
- Dehydration degrades decision-making before it becomes fatal
- Food is rarely urgent


### Exposure Overrides Everything

If environmental exposure is present (cold, heat, wind, wet conditions), shelter becomes the first priority regardless of other needs.


### Priorities Are Dynamic

Priorities change as conditions change.

- Daylight vs darkness
- Dry vs wet
- Stable weather vs incoming storm


### Time Compression

Available decision time may be shorter than expected.

- Loss of daylight
- Temperature drops
- Weather shifts

Early action is often more efficient than delayed action.


### Energy Conservation

All actions must be evaluated against energy cost.

- Avoid unnecessary movement
- Avoid overbuilding
- Avoid redundant work


## 3. Techniques

### Initial Priority Sequence

Upon recognizing a survival situation:

1. Apply STOP (from Chapter 1)
2. Assess immediate threats:
   - Injury
   - Exposure
   - Loss of orientation
3. Determine environmental risk:
   - Temperature
   - Wind
   - Precipitation
   - Remaining daylight


### Priority Decision Model

Use the following sequence:

1. **Shelter / Exposure Control**
   - Required if cold, wet, windy, or extreme heat

2. **Water**
   - Identify source early
   - Do not wait until dehydration is severe

3. **Signaling**
   - Establish ability to be found
   - Maintain signaling capability over time

4. **Movement**
   - Only if it improves position or safety

5. **Food**
   - Only after other systems are stable


### Environmental Overrides

#### Cold / Wet Conditions
- Shelter is immediate priority
- Fire becomes critical

#### Hot / Dry Conditions
- Water and shade are primary
- Movement should be minimized during peak heat

#### Limited Daylight
- Shelter must be established before darkness
- Navigation becomes secondary


### Time Blocking

Divide remaining daylight into blocks:

- Shelter construction time
- Fire setup time
- Water acquisition time

Avoid starting tasks that cannot be completed before dark.


## 4. Field Drills

### Drill: First Hour Response

- Simulate loss of orientation
- Set a timer for 60 minutes
- Execute:
  - STOP
  - Threat assessment
  - Shelter selection and start

Goal: reduce hesitation and wasted movement


### Drill: Environmental Shift

- Practice in changing conditions (e.g., approaching rain)
- Re-evaluate priorities every 15 minutes

Goal: reinforce dynamic prioritization


### Drill: Daylight Constraint

- Begin exercise with limited time before dark
- Force prioritization under time pressure


## 5. Common Mistakes

- Moving immediately after becoming disoriented
- Prioritizing navigation over shelter in poor conditions
- Underestimating environmental exposure
- Delaying water acquisition
- Attempting complex tasks with limited time


## 6. Decision Triggers

- Cold, wind, or wet conditions → build shelter immediately
- Less than 2 hours of daylight → begin shelter construction
- Signs of dehydration → locate and secure water
- Audible or visual rescue opportunity → shift to signaling


## 7. Equipment Notes

- Headlamp extends usable time but does not eliminate risk of night work
- Water container increases efficiency of collection
- Lightweight tarp significantly reduces shelter build time
