# Time, Energy, and Risk Management

## 1. Why It Matters

In a survival situation, failure is rarely caused by a single bad decision. It is typically the result of accumulated inefficiencies—wasted time, unnecessary movement, and unmanaged risk.

Time, energy, and risk are tightly linked. Mismanaging one degrades the others. Effective survival depends on controlling all three simultaneously.


## 2. Principles

### Time Is Non-Recoverable

Lost time cannot be regained. Delays in shelter construction, fire starting, or water acquisition increase exposure and reduce options.

- Act early when conditions are stable
- Avoid deferring critical tasks


### Energy Is Finite

Energy expenditure must be deliberate.

- Every action has a cost
- Fatigue reduces decision quality
- Overexertion leads to compounding failure


### Risk Compounds Over Time

Small risks accumulate.

- Minor exposure becomes hypothermia
- Mild dehydration reduces cognitive function
- Small navigation errors lead to disorientation


### Efficiency Over Output

The objective is not to do more. It is to do what matters with minimal cost.

- Smaller shelter built early is better than larger shelter built late
- Reliable fire method is better than complex method that may fail


### Avoid Irreversible Decisions

Actions that eliminate options should be avoided unless necessary.

- Descending terrain without a return path
- Crossing water without a recovery plan


## 3. Techniques

### Time Allocation Model

Divide available time into functional blocks:

- Shelter
- Fire
- Water
- Signaling

Estimate duration realistically and include buffer time.


### Energy Budgeting

Before starting a task, evaluate:

- Expected energy cost
- Expected benefit
- Probability of success

Prioritize tasks with high success probability and high return.


### Task Sequencing

Sequence tasks to reduce rework:

- Gather materials before construction
- Prepare tinder before attempting ignition
- Identify water before committing to camp location


### Risk Screening

For each action, assess:

- What can go wrong?
- What is the consequence?
- Can it be mitigated?

Avoid actions where failure has high consequence and low recovery.


### Micro-Decision Control

Break larger tasks into smaller decisions:

- Evaluate after each step
- Adjust approach as needed


## 4. Field Drills

### Drill: Time-Constrained Build

- Set a fixed time (e.g., 45 minutes)
- Build a functional shelter within that limit

Goal: improve time awareness and execution speed


### Drill: Energy Awareness

- Track perceived exertion during tasks
- Stop before exhaustion

Goal: develop awareness of energy limits


### Drill: Risk Evaluation

- During field activity, pause and identify risks in current plan

Goal: build habit of continuous risk assessment


## 5. Common Mistakes

- Starting tasks too late in the day
- Overexertion early in the scenario
- Attempting complex solutions under stress
- Ignoring small risks until they escalate
- Failing to reassess after partial progress


## 6. Decision Triggers

- Fatigue impacting coordination or judgment → stop and reassess
- Approaching darkness with incomplete shelter → stop other tasks
- Repeated failure of a method → change approach
- Increasing environmental risk → shift priorities immediately


## 7. Equipment Notes

- Lightweight tools reduce energy expenditure over time
- Reliable fire-starting tools reduce time and risk
- Simple systems outperform complex setups under stress
