# Survival Mindset and Decision-Making

## 1. Why It Matters

Most survival situations are not lost because of lack of tools or knowledge. They are lost because of poor decisions made under stress. Panic, hesitation, overconfidence, and fixation lead to wasted time, wasted energy, and increased exposure to risk.

The objective is not to perform perfectly. The objective is to make decisions that preserve life, conserve energy, and increase the probability of recovery.


## 2. Principles

### Control the Initial Response
The first 5–10 minutes after recognizing a problem are critical. Panic leads to movement without direction, which compounds risk.

- Stop immediately
- Breathe and stabilize
- Do not move until you have a plan

### Prioritize Survival Functions
Decisions must align to survival priorities:

1. Shelter / exposure control
2. Water
3. Signaling
4. Movement
5. Food

Food is rarely an immediate priority.

### Time and Energy Are Finite Resources
Every action has a cost. Unnecessary movement, poor shelter construction, or inefficient fire building can exhaust reserves quickly.

### Avoid Fixation
Do not commit to a failing approach.

- If a fire method is not working, change method
- If a route is unclear, stop and reassess

### Bias Toward Simplicity
Simple solutions are more reliable under stress.

- Smaller shelter vs complex structure
- Known water source vs speculative search

### Stay Oriented to Recovery
Survival is not isolation. The goal is to be found or to return.

- Maintain awareness of location
- Preserve ability to signal


## 3. Techniques

### The STOP Method

**Stop** — halt movement immediately

**Think** — assess the situation

**Observe** — evaluate environment, resources, risks

**Plan** — choose next actions based on priorities


### Decision Loop

Use a simple loop:

- Assess
- Decide
- Act
- Reassess

Repeat continuously. Do not assume initial decisions remain valid.


### Risk Assessment

Before acting, evaluate:

- What is the benefit?
- What is the risk?
- What is the energy cost?
- What happens if this fails?


### Movement Control

Do not move without purpose.

- Movement increases exposure and energy loss
- Movement without navigation increases risk of disorientation


## 4. Field Drills

### Drill: STOP Under Pressure

- Simulate being lost
- Immediately apply STOP
- Write down observations and plan

Goal: build automatic pause before action


### Drill: Decision Logging

- During a hike, periodically stop
- Document current priorities and decisions

Goal: improve awareness and structured thinking


### Drill: Limited Resource Scenario

- Assign constraints (limited water, time, daylight)
- Make and justify decisions based on constraints


## 5. Common Mistakes

- Continuing to move after becoming disoriented
- Prioritizing food too early
- Overbuilding shelter and wasting energy
- Ignoring environmental changes (weather, temperature)
- Fixating on one solution despite failure


## 6. Decision Triggers

- Loss of orientation → STOP immediately
- Temperature drop or approaching darkness → prioritize shelter
- Signs of dehydration → prioritize water
- Audible or visible rescue opportunity → prioritize signaling


## 7. Equipment Notes

- A watch improves time awareness and planning
- A notebook supports structured thinking under stress
- Navigation tools reduce decision uncertainty but do not replace discipline
